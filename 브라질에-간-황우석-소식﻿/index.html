Paulo Woo Suk Hwang, the South Korean scientist who led one of the biggest fraud scandals in the history of modern science, wants to develop research in Brazil, related to the areas of biomedicine, cosmetics and animal biotechnology – including the cloning of dogs and cattle. “Brazil has huge potential in biotechnology. We are talking to potential interested developers, including scientists and entrepreneurs, “Hwang said the State yesterday, exclusively, during a quick visit to the country He said it was his first interview with a foreign journalist (outside of Korea) in years . “The details I can only reveal in the near future,” he added, with a smile. Ten years ago, in February 2004, Hwang also had reason to smile, thanks to the publication of pioneering work in the journal Science , which reported first obtaining cloned human embryonic stem cells from an adult – an historic achievement, which brought with it the expectation of cure for a number of diseases. The following year, more reasons to smile with the publication of the first canine clone (a dog named Snuppy) and a second study that reported obtaining 11 other strains of human embryonic stem cells, cloned from patients with diabetes and other problems health. Was open, supposedly the era of “therapeutic cloning.” In late 2005, however, the happiness disappeared from the face of Hwang, where complaints and investigations revealed that the two works stem cells were a complete fraud. Hwang fell in scientific disgrace and was expelled from Seoul National University, but continued to be adored by the public in Korea and rose again from the ashes. Received $ 3.5 million in donations and, in 2006, created a new center of biotechnology research, the Sooam Biotech , whose most prominent (and source of funds) the commercial cloning of dogs. Over 600 adorable “dog pets” cloned have been produced in their labs, he said. The center also produces transgenic animals and works with cloning cows, pigs and dogs for research and possible therapeutic applications. Thus, Hwang smiled again. But ensure you are not satisfied. “I want another chance,” he says in melancholy, to prove that the science behind their research with embryonic stem cells was true. And I have a chance to use these cells to cure incurable diseases in the future. That’s my biggest and ultimate desire as a scientist, “he says. Hwang recognizes that both works were fabricated and doctored data and images, and deserved to be portrayed. The second, describing the 11 strains, was a “total fabrication,” he says, blaming a co-author “of another hospital.” Have the lineage of the first job he said was true. “I strongly believe that the first lineage that produced (published in 2004) was true. I agree that there were problems in the data and images, but I believe the nature of the cells was correct, they were actually human embryonic stem cells and cloned, “says Hwang. After the scandal, he says he voluntarily stopped doing research with human embryonic stem cells – among other reasons, because the South Korean government stopped approving their projects and give you money for it. But would love to resume them. And you expect people to trust him again as a scientist. “I really want to continue my research with human embryonic stem cells, the Korean society understand and agree with that. If the Korean government give me permission to resume these surveys, I guarantee you’ll do it with a more humble attitude, “he says. “I went through very difficult times, many difficulties in the past ten years. Credit: Reuters / Lee Jae-Won About Cloning dogsThe main activity of Sooam Biotech is to clone dogs. Some of them are produced with genetic alterations, to serve as animal models for the study of diseases – for example, beagles genetically modified (GM) to develop Alzheimer’s. Most, however, are pet dogs or with special skills, such as high performance-sniffing dogs used by Korean police. The service costs about $ 100,000, according to this report in the journal Nature : http://migre.me/jdBhH Ask Hwang to clone dogs is science or business: “When we cloned the first dog, Snuppy, in 2005, the success rate of cloning was less than 1%. Today, we spend 35%; so I can say that cloning dogs stopped being science and moved to the industrial scale, “he replies. Cows and pigs are more difficult to clone, with a success rate of 15% and 10%, respectively, due to biological and reproductive characteristics of each species. About cloning a mammoth’s most audacious project – and media – the center is currently cloning a frozen mammoth found in Siberia. Hwang says he was called to join the project because of its experience with the cloning of an endangered species of coyote, using a technique of “interspecies cloning”, which uses an animal of a different species (but genetically close) the one wishes to clone as egg donor and surrogacy. In the case of the coyote, ova and uterus of a bitch were used. In the case of frozen mammoth, the idea is to use a female Asian elephant – which, according to Hwang, the Myanmar government has agreed to provide. The first step in the process, he said, was recently given, with the extraction of intact cells (“in a very early stage of development”) of frozen muscle tissue of the animal. The next step is to place the nucleus of that cell into the egg of an elephant and make her turn an embryo. “I hope to initiate these experiments next year,” says Hwang. “But it’s just an expectation.” If being right, who knows, Hwang will be the first scientist to bring an extinct species of prehistory back to life. In the cattle industry, the center develops transgenic cows capable of producing molecules pharmacological interest in milk.
